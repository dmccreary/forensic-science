# Human or Beast? The Medullary Index Mystery

!!! mascot-welcome "Welcome, Investigators!"
    ![Trace waving welcome](../../img/mascot/welcome.png){ class="mascot-admonition-img"}

    A single hair can settle a huge question before you know anything else: did a
    person leave it, or did an animal? Get that wrong and the whole case wanders
    off course. Today you'll measure the hollow core running down a hair and let
    one ratio tell you human from beast. Sharpen your focus. Follow the evidence!

## The Case

A struggle. A victim found with a single **hair clutched in one hand** — pulled,
by the look of the root, from whoever or whatever they grabbed. Before the lab
spends weeks on DNA, the first question is the cheapest and fastest one to
answer: **is this hair even human?** The scene had a lot of foot traffic:

- The **victim's own dog**, a shedding shepherd mix, was in the room.
- A **wool blanket** was tangled at the scene.
- A **suspect** with medium-brown head hair was reported fleeing.

Your job: mount reference hairs from human, dog, cat, and wool, measure each
one's **medulla** and **shaft** under the microscope, compute the **medullary
index**, and classify the mystery hair — human or non-human — using the numbers,
not a guess.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this investigation you will be able to:

1. **Prepare** a wet-mount slide of a hair sample for compound microscopy.
2. **Measure** the medulla diameter and shaft diameter of a hair using a
   calibrated scale.
3. **Calculate** the medullary index as the ratio of medulla to shaft diameter.
4. **Classify** a hair as human or non-human by comparing its medullary index to
   reference ranges.

## Materials

Per group (≈ $15):

- Reference hairs: a human head hair (volunteer), dog fur, cat fur, and a strand
  of wool yarn
- 1 "mystery" hair (provided sealed by your teacher)
- Blank glass slides + coverslips
- Clear nail polish, glycerin, or water as a mountant
- Tweezers / forceps
- Ocular micrometer **or** a printed scale bar / calibrated reticle
- Lab notebook and calculator
- *Shared:* the AFIS-style Medullary Index Calculator MicroSim (below)
- *Assumes access to:* a classroom **compound microscope** (100×–400×)

!!! mascot-warning "Safety & Fair-Test Rules"
    ![Trace looking alert](../../img/mascot/warning.png){ class="mascot-admonition-img"}

    - Glass slides and coverslips **chip and cut** — handle by the edges and tell
      your teacher about any breakage. Goggles on while mounting.
    - If you use nail polish as a mountant, work in a ventilated area; the fumes
      are strong.
    - **Measure the same way every time** — always the widest part of the medulla
      and the widest part of the shaft at the same spot. Inconsistent measuring
      points are the number-one source of a wrong index.

## Background: The Hollow Core That Tells the Tale

Look down the length of a hair under magnification and you'll see three layers.
The outer **cuticle** is a shell of overlapping scales. Beneath it, the
**cortex** carries the pigment that gives hair its color. And running down the
center is the **medulla** — a channel of cells that may be a solid rod, a beaded
string, a wide hollow tube, or absent entirely. That central channel is the key
to this whole lab.

Forensic examiners quantify the medulla with the **medullary index**: the
diameter of the medulla divided by the diameter of the whole hair shaft.

$$\text{Medullary Index} = \frac{\text{medulla diameter}}{\text{shaft diameter}}$$

The rule of thumb is simple and powerful. In **human** hair the medulla is thin,
fragmentary, or missing, so the index is **less than about 0.33** — the medulla
takes up under a third of the shaft. In most **animal** hair the medulla is broad,
so the index is **0.5 or greater** — half the shaft or more. A wide, patterned
core screams *animal*; a narrow or absent one points to *human*.

A quick, honest caveat: hair is **class evidence**. The medullary index can tell
you human versus animal, and scale patterns can even narrow the species, but a
hair alone rarely identifies one individual person. That's a job for the DNA in
the root. The 2009 NAS report specifically criticized overstated microscopic
hair "matches," so we measure carefully and claim only what the number supports.
Practice the calculation before you touch a slide.

## Procedure

**Part 1 — Mount the reference hairs.**

1. Lay one reference hair (start with the known human head hair) flat on a clean
   slide. Add one drop of mountant and lower a coverslip at an angle to avoid
   trapping bubbles.
2. Repeat for the **dog**, **cat**, and **wool** references, one slide each.
   Label every slide.
3. Mount the sealed **mystery hair** on its own labeled slide the same way.

**Part 2 — Measure under the microscope.**

4. Focus on a reference hair at medium power. Using your ocular micrometer or
   scale bar, measure the **shaft diameter** (full width of the hair) at a clear
   spot.
5. At the **same spot**, measure the **medulla diameter** (width of the central
   channel). If no medulla is visible, record it as 0.
6. Record both numbers for every reference hair and for the mystery hair. Note
   the medulla's **appearance** too — solid, beaded, ladder-like, or absent.

**Part 3 — Calculate and classify.**

7. For each hair, compute the **medullary index** = medulla ÷ shaft. Check your
   value in the MicroSim.
8. Compare each index to the reference ranges: **< 0.33 → human**, **≥ 0.5 →
   animal**, **0.33–0.5 → inconclusive, remeasure and use scale pattern.**
9. Classify the **mystery hair** and decide which scene source it is consistent
   with — the suspect, the dog, or the wool blanket.

## Analysis Questions

1. What is the medullary index of the mystery hair, and how does it classify —
   human or animal? Cite the number and the range it falls in.
2. Which scene source (suspect, dog, or wool) is the mystery hair **consistent
   with**? Which sources can you **exclude**?
3. Compare the medulla *appearance* of your human hair to your animal hairs.
   Describe two visible differences beyond the index number itself.
4. A hair gives a medullary index of **0.40** — right in the gray zone. What two
   things should you do before reporting a classification?
5. Hair is **class evidence**. The 2009 NAS report criticized overstated hair
   "matches." Explain why your report can say the hair is *consistent with* a
   source but should **not** say it came from one specific individual.

## Deliverable

Turn in a one-page **Hair Analysis Report** that gives the mystery hair's
medullary index, its human/animal classification, and which scene source it is
consistent with (and which it excludes). Include your full data table and a note
on any measurement you'd repeat to be sure.

!!! mascot-tip "Investigator Tip"
    ![Trace holding a magnifying glass](../../img/mascot/tip.png){ class="mascot-admonition-img"}

    Measure the medulla and the shaft at the **exact same point** along the hair,
    and use the widest clear view of each. A medulla that beads and pinches will
    hand you three different indices in three different spots — pick a
    representative location and be consistent, or your ratio is fiction.

??? question "Extension Challenge: Read the Scales"
    The medullary index isn't the only clue. Make a **scale cast** by pressing a
    hair into a thin layer of clear nail polish on a slide, letting it dry, and
    peeling the hair away to leave a scale imprint. Human scales look flattened
    and irregular; many animals show crown-like (coronal) or spiny (petal)
    scales. Cast two of your samples and describe how the scale pattern *confirms*
    your index-based classification.

## Teacher Notes

??? note "Setup, timing, and grading (click to expand)"
    - **Prep:** Collect reference hairs ahead of time — a volunteer head hair,
      brushed dog and cat fur, and a single-ply wool strand. Choose a mystery
      hair that gives a clear, non-borderline index so groups get a decisive
      result their first time out.
    - **Calibration:** If you have ocular micrometers, calibrate them against a
      stage micrometer before class. If not, a printed scale bar photographed at
      known magnification works for relative ratios (the index is unit-free, so
      consistent units are all you need).
    - **Differentiation:** For a shorter version, pre-mount the reference slides
      and have groups measure only. For a challenge, add a second mystery hair
      that lands in the 0.33–0.5 gray zone to force a remeasure-and-scale-pattern
      decision.
    - **Assessment focus:** Reward consistent measuring points, a correctly
      computed index, and cautious class-evidence language — *consistent with*, not
      *identical to*. Flag any report that claims a single individual from a hair.
