# Locard's Silent Witness — Trace Evidence Transfer

!!! mascot-welcome "Welcome, Investigators!"
    ![Trace waving welcome](../../img/mascot/welcome.png){ class="mascot-admonition-img"}

    Somebody leaned against the wrong thing at the wrong time — and they have
    no idea what they left behind. In this investigation you'll prove the most
    important rule in all of forensic science: **every contact leaves a trace.**
    Grab your tape and your magnifier. Follow the evidence!

## The Case

Last night someone leaned against the freshly cleaned display case in the school
museum and pocketed a rare coin. The case was wiped spotless beforehand, so
**anything on it now was left by the thief.** Three suspects were in the building:

- **Suspect A** wore a red fleece jacket.
- **Suspect B** wore a wool scarf.
- **Suspect C** carried a folder dusted with craft glitter from art class.

Your job: examine the "display case" surface, recover the trace evidence, and
decide which suspect made contact — using **evidence, not the story.**

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this investigation you will be able to:

1. **Explain** Locard's Exchange Principle in your own words.
2. **Recover** trace evidence from a surface using a tape lift.
3. **Document** the *two-way* (bidirectional) transfer between two objects.
4. **Use** recovered trace evidence to include or exclude a suspect.

## Materials

Per group (≈ $18):

- Clear packing tape + white index cards (for tape lifts)
- 1 red fleece glove or fabric square
- 1 wool scarf or wool yarn
- Craft glitter
- A small handful of pet fur or craft fur
- 5× hand magnifiers or loupes
- Tweezers
- A black construction-paper "display case" mat
- *Shared:* one classroom compound microscope for the reveal

!!! mascot-warning "Safety & Fair-Test Rules"
    ![Trace looking alert](../../img/mascot/warning.png){ class="mascot-admonition-img"}

    - **Wipe the mat clean** before every trial — a contaminated scene is a
      ruined scene.
    - Handle tape lifts by the **edges only**. Your own sleeve is now a source
      of contamination.
    - No eating the glitter. Yes, Trace has to say it every year.

## Background: The Principle That Started It All

In the early 1900s, French scientist **Edmond Locard** argued that a criminal
always brings something to a scene and always takes something away. We now call
this **Locard's Exchange Principle**, and it is the reason trace evidence exists
at all.

The transfer goes **both ways**. If a suspect's wool scarf brushes the display
case, wool fibers move *onto the case* — and microscopic traces from the case
move *onto the scarf*. A good investigator looks for **both** halves of the
exchange. Fibers, hairs, and glitter are **class evidence**: they can tell you a
suspect is *consistent with* the scene, and they can *exclude* a suspect
outright, but they rarely identify one single person by themselves.

Before you touch anything, meet the principle in action.

## Procedure

**Part 1 — Stage the exchange (the "crime").**

1. One team member is the **Scene Keeper**. Wipe the black mat completely clean.
2. A second member is the **Suspect**. *Without the analysts watching*, press
   **one** suspect object (fleece, wool, or glitter folder) firmly against the
   mat for five seconds. Set it aside in a labeled bag.
3. The Scene Keeper seals the mat — this is now your protected crime scene.

**Part 2 — Recover the trace evidence.**

4. Press a strip of clear tape onto the mat, lift it, and stick it to a **white**
   index card. Label it **"SCENE."**
5. Take a tape lift from **each of the three suspect objects** and label them
   **A, B, and C.** These are your knowns.

**Part 3 — Compare.**

6. Examine every tape lift with a magnifier, then under the microscope.
7. Record what you see: fiber color, thickness, curl, and any glitter or fur.
8. Look for the **two-way match** — the material on the SCENE lift should also
   appear on exactly one suspect's lift, *and* trace from the mat should appear
   on that suspect's object.

## Data Collection

Fill in one row per tape lift.

| Lift | Fibers seen (color / type) | Glitter? | Hair/fur? | Matches SCENE? |
|------|----------------------------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| SCENE | | | | — |
| Suspect A (fleece) | | | | |
| Suspect B (wool) | | | | |
| Suspect C (glitter) | | | | |

## Analysis Questions

1. Which suspect's known lift best matches the SCENE lift? Cite **two**
   observations that support your conclusion.
2. Give one piece of evidence that lets you **exclude** a different suspect.
3. Describe the **second half** of the exchange — what would you expect to find
   *on the suspect's object* that came from the mat?
4. Fibers are **class evidence**. Explain why matching fibers alone would not be
   enough to convict in a real courtroom.
5. Where in your procedure was contamination most likely to sneak in, and how
   did you guard against it?

## Deliverable

Turn in a one-page **Trace Evidence Report** that states which suspect you
include, which you exclude, and the two-way evidence that supports each
decision. Attach your labeled tape-lift cards.

!!! mascot-thinking "What Does the Data Tell Us?"
    ![Trace peering through a magnifying glass](../../img/mascot/thinking.png){ class="mascot-admonition-img"}

    A match makes a suspect **consistent with** the scene — not automatically
    guilty. The strongest reports say exactly what the evidence *can* prove and
    are honest about what it *can't*. That precision is what separates a real
    investigator from a TV detective.

??? question "Extension Challenge: The Double Transfer"
    Design a trial where **two** suspects touch the mat. Can your team still
    tell them apart from a single SCENE lift? What extra documentation would you
    need to keep their contributions straight? Write a short plan and test it.

## Teacher Notes

??? note "Setup, timing, and grading (click to expand)"
    - **Prep:** Pre-cut tape strips and index cards. Pre-load each suspect object
      with a distinct, easy-to-see trace (bright fleece, dark wool, chunky
      glitter) so microscope time stays short.
    - **The reveal:** Keep the "which object touched the mat" answer in a sealed
      envelope. Groups commit to a conclusion *before* opening it — this rewards
      evidence over guessing.
    - **Differentiation:** For a quicker version, skip the microscope and judge
      with magnifiers only. For a challenge, add a decoy object with fibers
      similar to a real suspect's.
    - **Assessment focus:** Reward students who describe **both** directions of
      transfer and who correctly distinguish *inclusion* from *identification*.
